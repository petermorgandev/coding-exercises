The Game Plan

Step 1: Don't lose.

White, Blue, and Black EDH decks, like this one, have access to cards that allow you to manipulate what happens in a game. These cards gives you more control over what your opponents can do as well as influence them to make decisions that are beneficial to you.

Ghostly Prison is an example of a card that changes how your opponents will play the game. They have to choose between using their mana to play their spells and attack other players or use their mana to attack you. Rhystic Study has a similar effect. Every your opponents spend to prevent you from drawing a card is less that they have to advance their own game plan.

There will be times in every game when your opponents have enough of a presence that you get worried that you may be in danger of losing no matter how much protection you have. It could be that they created a flying battalion of Angel tokens or are setting up a combination of cards that will win them the game. This is the perfect opportunity to play 1 of the 7 board wipes in the deck to clear the board and make it easier for us to win.

Step 2: Win.

Not loosing is good. It's the first step toward winning. However, just because you're not losing does not mean that you are also winning. This deck has 2 main avenues of winning instant win combos and politics. Instant win combos, which I discuss in the next section, can end the game out of nowhere. They require some preparation, but can be hard to stop once you get them going.

Another way to win is by using politics and bluffing to your advantage. With your defenses set up it can be discouraging for your opponents to attack you. There is also no gaurentee that they will attack each other, so you may need to entice them to do so. This is where bluffing can be useful. You can say something to one of your opponents like "I have a board wipe, but I will wait a turn to play it if you attack someone else on your turn." You can say this even if you don't have a board wipe in your hand because your opponents can't typically see what cards you have in your hand.

If everything goes your way you can wait until all of your opponents are low on life and then attack with your creatures or cast a card like Debt to the Deathless to finish the game.

I Win Now Combos

  • Diabolic Tutor
  • Exquisite Blood
  • Felidar Sovereign
  • Mikaeus, the Unhallowed
  • Obzedat's Aid
  • Sanguine Bond
  • Triskelion
  • Vedalken Orrery

As much fun as the experience of playing Magic is, the goal of the game is to win. This deck includes a number of card combinations (combos) that together can win the game by dealing infinite damage, gaining infinite life, or winning on the spot. Combos can be hard to assemble as there are multiple parts to each combo and the combo can be easily disrupted before you win. Diabolic Tutor is in the deck to help you find the missing card that you need to complete the combo and Vedalken Orrery allows you to put pieces of the combo onto the battlefield at the end of your opponents' turn to give them as little time as possible to disrupt it. In case one of the pieces of a combo is in the graveyard you can use Obzedat's Aid to return it to the battlefield.

The combination of Exquisite Blood and Sanguine Bond gains you infinite life while your opponents lose infinite life. The combo starts when you gain life or your opponent loses life. That is very easy to do with the large number of lifegain cards in the deck. Even Oloro's triggered ability can start this combo.

On the topic of gaining life, you want to keep your life total above 40 so that you can win the game with Felidar Sovereign on the battlefield at the beginning of your upkeep. You don't need any other cards to win this way, but it does help to have Vedalken Orrery on the battlefield when you want to play this cat beast.

The second 2 card combo is from an interaction between Mikaeus, the Unhallowed and Triskelion. This combo deals infinite damage to your opponents by using Triskelion activated ability in combination with the Undying and +1/+1 given by Mikaeus, the Unhallowed. You remove counters from Triskelion to deal damage to one of your opponents. Then you remove counters to deal enough damage to Triskelion that it kills itself. It then returns to the battlefield because it has Undying. You repeat the process until you have dealt enough damage to your opponents to win the game. Typically you can demonstrate this combo once and your opponents will concede.

Planeswalkers

  • Jace, Architect of Thought
  • Ob Nixilis of the Black Oath
  • Sorin Markov
  • Teferi, Temporal Archmage
  • Ugin, the Spirit Dragon

Planeswalkers first appeared in Lorwyn and act differently than other cards. Each turn you can activate one of the planeswalker's abilities by paying its cost in loyalty counters. They are similar to legendary creatures in that you cannot have, for example, multiple Jace planeswalkers on the battlefield at the same time. Planeswalkers can be attacked by other players and dealt damage in the form of loyalty counter loss. While planeswalkers can be vulnerable to attack, in an ideal scenario these planeswalkers can stay on the board for a longer-than-average amount of time due to the number of board wipes and protection cards in the deck.

Jace has, in many ways, been one of the faces of Magic over the last few years. His planeswalker card from Return to Ravnica was one of the first planeswalker cards that I opened after returning to the game. Similar to Teferi, Temporal Archmage and Ugin, the Spirit Dragon, Jace, Architect of Thought does a little bit of everything.

The 2 Black planeswalker cards in the deck provide synergy with other non-paneswalker cards. Both Ob Nixilis of the Black Oath and Sorin Markov cause your opponents to lose life while gaining you life. Ob Nixilis can also create creatures which is helpful considering the number of creature cards in the deck is lower than average. Using Sorin's -3 ability when Blood Baron of Vizkopa is on the battlefield is especially fun as you can often take out a player who, until that turn, had a very high life total.

Board Wipes

  • Black Sun's Zenith
  • Cyclonic Rift
  • Day of Judgment
  • Magister of Worth
  • Merciless Eviction
  • Nevinyrral's Disk
  • Supreme Verdict

Board wipes are cards that have the ability to remove many, or all, cards from the battlefield. They are an important part of an EDH deck as the cards people play are very powerful and sometimes difficult to deal with if they stay on the battlefield for more than a few turns. Day of Judgment is the most basic example of a board wipe. If it resolves, you destroy all creatures on the battlefield. Supreme Verdict has the same effect, but has the added benefit of not being able to be countered. Nevinyrral's Disk steps it up a notch by also destroying all artifacts and enchantments while Magister of Worth can ensure that you will still have a creature left on the battlefield when everyone else's are destroyed.

The cards that I have highlighted so far in this section are good for destroying creatures, but what do you do if a creature is indestructible or can be brought back to the battlefield from the graveyard. Black Sun's Zenith solves the first problem by placing -1/-1 counters on all creatures as a creature will die if it has toughness less than 1. Merciless Eviction exiles all artifacts, creatures, enchantments, or planeswalkers ensuring that the cards of the type that you chose are gone for good. If that's not good enough you can cast Cyclonic Rift by paying its Overload cost to return all nonland permanents that you don't control back to their owner's hand.

Mana Ramp

  • Chromatic Lantern
  • Commander's Sphere
  • Crypt Ghast
  • Darksteel Ingot
  • Pristine Talisman
  • Sol Ring
  • Worn Powerstone

One of the fundamental rules of Magic is that you can only put one land card on the battlefield during each of your turns. Some cards, like Exploration and Summer Bloom allow you to play multiple land cards from your hand on the same turn. Other cards like Cultivate and Farseek let you search your library for land cards and put them onto the battlefield. These cards are all Green cards, which means we cannot play them in this deck.

Even though we cannot include cards in this deck that allow us to put more lands into play, there are plenty of artifacts that we can tap for mana. Sol Ring and Worn Powerstone tap for and Pristine Talisman taps for while gaining you 1 life.

Chromatic Lantern, Commander's Sphere, and Darksteel Ingot tap for 1 mana of any color. While they are similar, they are different. Chromatic Lantern lets you tap your lands for any color of mana. For example, with Chromatic Lantern on the battlefield you can cast Counterspell by tapping 2 Plains to produce . With Crypt Ghast on the battlefield at the same time you can tap each Swamp for a mana of any color and get an additional . Going one step further, with Urborg, Tomb of Yawgmoth on the battlefield every land you control taps for any color of mana and produces an additional .

Commander's Sphere gives you the option of sacrificing it to draw a card. This is especially useful when an opponent cast a board wipe like Planar Cleansing or Vandalblast. The Indestructible keyword on Darksteel Ingot gives it protection from those kind of board wipes.

Card Draw

  • Erebos, God of the Dead
  • Notion Thief
  • Phyrexian Arena
  • Rhystic Study
  • Underworld Connections

Drawing more cards than just the one you get during your draw step is essential in EDH as the cards played in the format are typically more powerful than the cards that see play in Standard or Modern. Drawing extra cards allows you to find the threats you need and the answers to the threats that your opponents are playing. While the best draw spell in Magic is banned in the format, there are plenty of other cards that enable you to draw additional cards.

This deck plays cards that allow me to draw cards in various ways. Oloro himself has a built in ability to draw extra cards when he is on the battlefield. Whenever I gain life with Oloro on the battlefield, I can pay and draw a card. This is a similar effect as having Phyrexian Arena in play. The difference being that my opponents lose life instead of me. Erebos, God of the Dead and Underworld Connections are also similar to Phyrexian Arena, thought their activations are different.

Drawing extra cards on your own turn is fine, but wouldn't it be nice to also draw cards on your opponents turn? Rhystic Study gives you the opportunity to draw a card whenever your opponents play a spell. My friend introduced me to this card when he played it in his own Esper deck and has drawn him countless cards as most players don't bother paying the to prevent him from drawing.

Finishing up the card draw section is Notion Thief. From the poorly received Dragon's Maze set, Notion Thief stops your opponents from drawing extra cards and allows you to draw the cards instead. Surprise your opponents by casting this with their draw spell or ability on the stack and revel in their misfortune.

  • Sphere of Safety
  • Swiftfoot Boots
  • Utter End
  • Worship

This is the largest section of cards, excluding the lands, and for good reason. Protecting yourself is vital with so few traditional win conditions in the deck. These cards can be divided into cards that protect you and the cards that protect your cards on the battlefield.

Starting with cards that protect you we have Counterspell and Render Silent. These 2 spells can prevent your opponents from resolving a powerful spell or ensuring that your own spell resolves. Render Silent has the added benefit of preventing the caster of the countered spell for the rest of the turn. Grand Abolisher has a similar effect during your turn as none of your opponents can cast spells or activate abilities.

In a format where you cannot have more than 1 of any nonbasic land card, there are 2 sets of cards in the deck that are functionally the same. Blind Obedience, Frozen Aether, and Kismet force your opponents' permanents to enter the battlefield tapped. Citadel Siege, while not exactly the same, allows you to tap a creature during each of your opponents' combat steps. The second set of cards make it harder for your opponents to attack you. Ghostly Prison, Norn's Annex, Propaganda, and Sphere of Safety require your opponents to pay mana for each creature that they want to attack you with.

Rounding out the cards that protect you we have Silent Arbiter, Utter End, and Worship. These cards don't have anything in common with each other except that they all help keep you in the game and disrupt what your opponents want to do.

Moving onto the cards that protect your other cards we start with Avacyn, Angel of Hope. If a flying indestructible 8/8 wasn't enough, it also makes all of your other permanents indestructible. To protect Avacyn, or any of the creatures in the deck, the deck includes EDH all star Swiftfoot Boots. Finally. in the event that one of your noncreature permanents is destroyed, Karmic Justic can destroy a permanent that the destroyer controls.

Lifegain

  • Alhammarret’s Archive
  • Blood Baron of Vizkopa
  • Boon Reflection
  • Debt to the Deathless
  • Divinity of Pride
  • Pontiff of Blight
  • Vizkopa Guildmage

Choosing the commander for your deck can push you towards utilizing certain strategies and synergies when constructing your deck. Oloro, Ageless Ascetic allows you to chance to draw cards every time that you gain life. Because we want to draw more cards it is a good idea to include multiple ways to gain life. Cards like Alhammarret’s Archive and Boon Reflection double the amount of life that we gain. With one of those on the battlefield in a 4 player game you could cast Debt to the Deathless for and gain 24 life while each of your opponents lose 12 life. The Extort ability on Pontiff of Blight performs a similar function as it allows you to gain life while

Creatures with the Lifelink ability can also gain you life when they deal combat damage. Blood Baron of Vizkopa and Divinity of Pride are both creatures with Lifelink that have evasive abilities which make them hard to block. If those aren't effective enough Vizkopa Guildmage can temporarily grant Lifelink to a creature that you control.

Misc

  • Grave Titan
  • Serra Avatar
  • Solemn Simulacrum
  • Steel Hellkite
  • Thassa, God of the Sea

This selection of cards didn't seem to fit into the other sections, or could fit into multiple sections. For example, Solemn Simulacrum has the roles of mana ramp and card draw. All of the cards are creatures, but there isn't one defining characteristic that ties them together. Steel Hellkite can be a mini-board wipe, and Serra Avatar wants you to have a high life total, but they don't fit into those specific categories.

Then you have Grave Titan and Thassa, God of the Sea. Both cards do so much by themselves. Thassa, God of the Sea gives you a repeatable Scry which allows you to more easily find the cards that you need to get ahead. You can also make creatures unblockable, which is a nice bonus. Grave Titan is an absurdly powerful card. For you add 10 power to the board. Its zombie producing ability makes it a great attacking creatures and Deathtouch makes it an even better blocker.

What is Magic: The Gathering?

Magic: The Gathering is a trading card game created by Wizards of the Coast. Players create decks of cards and battle their friends in games involving decision-making, strategy, and a little bit of luck. Games of Magic are played all over the world from kitchen tables to the highest level of competitive tournaments.

What is EDH?

EDH, or Commander, is a Magic: The Gathering format played with 100 card decks. Each deck has a designated commander and cannot have more than one copy of any card with the exception of basic lands cards.
